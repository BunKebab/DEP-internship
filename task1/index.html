Introduction to Chess

Chess is a strategic board game that has been played for centuries. It is a two-player game where each player controls an army of pieces with the goal of checkmating the opponent's king. Originating in India around the 6th century, chess has evolved into a game of skill and strategy enjoyed worldwide.

Pieces

Pawn

Moves forward one square but captures diagonally. On its first move, it can move forward two squares.

Knight

Moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and then one square perpendicular. Can jump over pieces.

Bishop

Moves any number of squares diagonally.

Rook

Moves any number of squares vertically or horizontally.

Queen

Moves any number of squares in any direction.

King

Moves one square in any direction.

How to play

  • Setup: The chessboard is set up with each player's pieces on the two rows closest to them. The back row should be Rook, Knight, Bishop, Queen, King, Bishop, Knight, Rook from left to right, and pawns on the row in front.
  • Objective: The objective is to checkmate the opponent's king, putting it under threat of capture with no legal moves to escape.
  • Special moves
    • Castling: Move the king two squares towards a rook, and the rook moves to the square over which the king crossed.
    • En Passant: A pawn capturing a pawn that has moved two squares forward from its starting position.
    • Promotion: A pawn reaching the opponent's back row can be promoted to any other piece, typically a queen.

Strategies

  • Control the Center: Dominating the central squares allows greater mobility for your pieces.
  • Develop Your Pieces: Move your knights and bishops out early to control the board.
  • King Safety: Ensure your king is safe, typically by castling early.
  • Plan Ahead: Think several moves ahead and anticipate your opponent's moves.

Chess notation and terms

Chess notation is a system used to record and describe the moves in a game. Each square on the board is identified by a unique coordinate. For example, the bottom-left square is a1, and the top-right square is h8. Moves are recorded with the piece's abbreviation followed by the destination square. For example, Nf3 means a knight moves to the f3 square.

Common terms
  • Check: The king is under threat of capture.
  • Checkmate: The king is in check and cannot escape.
  • Stalemate: The game ends in a draw as a player has no legal moves but is not in check.
